Project 1:
Mobile CORE

My first project at MIM was to create mobile version of the CORE index page and design the filtering and sorting feature to adapt the mobile environment.






Brainstorm Card View Layout


I started brainstorming some layout ideas by sketching and quick wireframing. After discussing with team, I was able to narrow down to 4 layouts and decided to go with the version 4 for two reasons: 1) the style matches with CORE as grey is the main color; 2) the design is easy to view where the top section contains the most important information such as task title, deadline and status, and the bottom section includes the rest of the information.

Iterate On the Card Design


Different from web browser, too much information on mobile devices could be overwhelming. After having the general layout of the card, I needed to decide what information to keep or exclude. Working with the engineer team who is one of the major users of CORE, I further iterated on the card design and prioritized information on the card based on user's needs.







Design Filter and Sort







Overall flow


The final step was to put pieces together into a flow. Before making the overall flow, I used grid to make sure the elements are aligned and followed the MIM design guideline of having 5-pixel-interval spaces between each element. I also made sure that interactive buttons and links have minimum touch target size and buttons are within the thumb zone.









Project 2: Dashboard Redesign

I also designed two new sections on the dashboard: To Do List and Favorites to address two users' pain points: 1) no reminder of upcoming tasks until they are overdue; 2) spend long time to find items users visit the most often.





To do list


On the CORE dashboard, users are not able to see what due dates are coming up until the overdue items appear on the top of the dashboard. I wanted to design a to do list that reminds users what deadlines are approaching. After some research on how users usually deal with deadlines and how important they are, I realized that for some users, deadlines are not specified in the task description but it still needs to be done within a time frame, while for users like engineers, deadlines are really important. To fulfill the need both user groups, I started exploring different approaches for user-directed prioritization.




After discussions with the team, I decided to make more detailed mockup based on the first version. The empty state maximizes personalization, giving users freedom to create their own to-do list.









Favorites


Currently in CORE, users are able to mark institutions that the company works with favorite and the list of marked items appears in the employee profile page. The design team suggested to enable the favorite feature not limited to institutions but to other items such as tasks, activities, tickets and so on. Once I settled down with creating To Do List on dashboard, I started exploring different ways Favorites could be presented. I experimented on few options:



I made decision to put Favorites in Dashboard for quick view and in a separate page accessed from the side bar navigation for editing by answering two questions: 1) When do users need to use Favorites; 2) Where provides the quick and easy access to Favorites.

Project 3: Dialpad Caller Project

Collaborating with engineers and customer support team, I designed the Dialpad Caller modal that helps customer support team match the incoming calls with suggested customers, so users don't have to search for the caller manually in CORE. This feature will save the searching time and lead to a more effective communication with the customer.





The process at a glance







Solution







A. Logo on top navigation opens the modal.
B. Caller ID and phone number are provided. When the caller doesn't match any of the suggested people/institution, customer support team can search in the CORE system using the search buttons.
C. When a call comes in, CORE will recognize a call event occurs, run in the SQL database and extract a list of potential people and institutions that are associated with the call.
D. When the match is confirmed, users will add note to record the customer's concerns.

Takeaway



Get feedback early and often


During the first few weeks of the internship, I was afraid to show my ideas/sketches in the early stage of the design process. Before progress checks-ins with the design lead, I always wanted to have at least some simple mock-ups ready to show. However, sometimes I didn't realize that I went to the wrong direction until finishing building the mock-ups. Getting feedback in the early stage could be impactful. It helped me to better understand the problem and iterate more effectively.



Learn how to communicate with different audiences


Design involves interdisciplinary groups. I've worked with engineers, data analyst, and customer support team. I learned how to convey my idea in various ways to different audience, such as changing use of language or walking through a user scenario.



Be able to explain rationale behind design choices


Each design choice has a reason behind it; it could be based on user research, design principles, or user behavior. Thinking through the rationale behind design choices helped me to make better decision .
