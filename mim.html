The process


Brainstorm Card View Layout


I started brainstorming card view layout ideas by sketching and quick wireframing and narrowed down to four versions. After discussing with team, I decided to go with the version 4 for two reasons: 1) the style matches with CORE as grey is the main color; 2) the design is easy to view where the top section contains the most important information such as task title, deadline and status, and the bottom section includes the rest of the information.

Iterate On the Card Design


Different from web browser, too much information on mobile devices could be overwhelming. After deciding the general layout of the card, I needed to decide what information to keep or exclude. Working with the engineer team who is one of the major users of CORE, I further iterated on the card design and prioritized information on the card based on user's needs.

Project 2: Dashboard Redesign

I also designed two new sections on the dashboard: To Do List and Favorites to address two users' pain points: 1) no reminder of upcoming tasks until they are overdue; 2) spend long time to find items users visit the most often.



To do list


On the CORE dashboard, users are not able to see what due dates are coming up until the overdue items appear on the top of the dashboard. I wanted to design a to do list that reminds users what deadlines are approaching. After some research on how users usually deal with deadlines and how important they are, I realized that for some users, deadlines are not specified in the task description but it still needs to be done within a time frame, while for users like engineers, deadlines are really important. To fulfill the need both user groups, I started exploring different approaches for user-directed prioritization.




After discussions with the team, I decided to make more detailed mockup based on the first version. The empty state maximizes personalization, giving users freedom to create their own to-do list.









Favorites


Currently in CORE, users are able to mark institutions that the company works with favorite and the list of marked items appears in the employee profile page. The design team suggested to enable the favorite feature not limited to institutions but to other items such as tasks, activities, tickets and so on. Project 3: Dialpad Caller Project

Collaborate with engineers and customer support team, I designed the Dialpad Caller modal that helps customer support team match the incoming calls with suggested customers, so users don't have to search for the caller manually in CORE. This feature will save the searching time and lead to a more effective communication with the customer.



The process at a glance


Solution







A. Logo on top navigation opens the modal.
B.Caller ID and phone number are provided. When the caller doesn't match any of the suggested people/institution, customer support team can search in the CORE system using the search buttons.
C. When a call comes in, CORE will recognize a call event occurs, run in the SQL database and extract a list of potential people and institutions that are associated with the call.
D. When the match is confirmed, users will add note to record the customer's concerns.

Takeaway


Get feedback early and often

During the first few weeks of the internship, I was afraid to show my ideas/sketches in the early stage of the design process. Before progress checks-ins with the design lead, I always wanted to have at least some simple mock-ups ready to show. However, sometimes I didn't realize that I went to the wrong direction until finishing building the mock-ups. Get feedback in the early stage could be impactful. It helped me to better understand the problem and iterate more effectively.


Learn how to communicate with different audiences

Design involves interdisciplinary groups. I've worked with engineers, data analyst, and customer support team. I learned how to convey my idea in various ways to different audience, such as changing use of language or walking through a user scenario.


Be able to explain rationale behind design choices

Each design choice has a reason; it could be based on user research, design principles, or user behavior. Think through the rationale behind each design choices helped to make better decision .
